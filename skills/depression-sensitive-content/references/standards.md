# Depression-Sensitive Content Standards

## Overview

This document outlines the professional standards and guidelines for creating emotionally safe content for people affected by depression.

## Key Standards

### APA Guidelines (American Psychological Association)

The APA recommends:
- **Person-first language**: "person with depression" NOT "depressed person"
- **Identity-conscious language when preferred**: Some individuals prefer "deaf person" over "person who is deaf"
- **Avoid pathologizing language**: Depression is a "condition" or "experience," not a "disease" in casual contexts

Reference: https://www.apa.org/topics/depression

### DBS Alliance Language Guide

The Depression and Bipolar Support Alliance recommends avoiding:
- "Suffering from," "afflicted with," "victim of"
- "Crazy," "insane," "psycho"
- "Commits suicide" (use "dies by suicide")
- "Mental illness" as a noun

Reference: https://www.dbsalliance.org/

### SPRC Guidelines (Suicide Prevention Resource Center)

When discussing suicide:
- Use "died by suicide" or "took their own life"
- Avoid "committed suicide" (implies crime/sin)
- Include crisis resources when relevant

Reference: https://www.sprc.org/

### WCAG 2.1 Emotional Safety

From a cognitive load perspective:
- Simple sentence structures reduce anxiety
- Clear, direct language is less overwhelming
- Positive framing supports hope

### ISO 26000 (Social Responsibility)

Guidelines on communicating about mental health:
- Avoid stigmatizing language
- Promote inclusion
- Respect dignity

## Clinical Terminology Boundaries

| Term | Use When | Avoid When |
|------|----------|------------|
| Depression | Clinical context, discussing diagnosis | Casual conversation, non-clinical contexts |
| Major depressive disorder | Professional/medical writing | General content |
| Feeling down | Casual contexts | Clinical contexts |
| Mental health condition | Formal writing | When "depression" is more precise |
