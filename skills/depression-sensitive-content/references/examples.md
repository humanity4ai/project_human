# Examples: Good vs Bad

## Example 1: Person-First Language

### Bad
"The depressed patient needs to follow the treatment plan."

### Good
"The person living with depression may find it helpful to follow the treatment plan."

### Why
Person-first language respects dignity. "May find it helpful" respects autonomy.

---

## Example 2: Avoiding Shame

### Bad
"Many people with depression are lazy and just need to try harder."

### Good
"Depression can make it hard to find motivation. Small steps can help."

### Why
Shame increases suffering and doesn't help. Validating the experience while offering hope is effective.

---

## Example 3: Crisis Language

### Bad
"I can't take this anymore. I just want to end it all."

**Response**: "That's great that you're reaching out. Keep going!"

### Good
"I hear you, and I'm concerned. Please reach out to 988 or text HOME to 741741."

### Why
Minimizing crisis language can be dangerous. Validation + resources saves lives.

---

## Example 4: Cognitive Accessibility

### Bad
"Depression, which is characterized by persistent feelings of sadness, loss of interest in activities that were previously enjoyed, changes in appetite and sleep patterns, difficulty concentrating, and feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt, affects millions of people worldwide."

### Good
"Depression affects millions of people. It can make you feel sad, lose interest in things, and have trouble concentrating. But there is help available."

### Why
Long, complex sentences increase cognitive load. Breaking into shorter sentences helps.

---

## Example 5: Avoiding Clinical Jargon

### Bad
"The patient exhibits non-compliance with pharmacotherapeutic interventions."

### Good
"The person isn't taking their medication as prescribed."

### Why
Clinical jargon is confusing and can feel impersonal.

---

## Example 6: Positive Framing

### Bad
"Don't let depression ruin your life."

### Good
"You can take steps toward feeling better."

### Why
"Don't" language can feel admonishing. Positive framing supports hope.

---

## Example 7: Empathy Without Pity

### Bad
"Poor thing, they must be so miserable."

### Good
"That sounds really difficult. How can I support you?"

### Why
Pity is condescending. Empathy validates without diminishing.

---

## Example 8: Hope-Oriented

### Bad
"Depression is a chronic condition that most people struggle with forever."

### Good
"With the right support, many people with depression lead fulfilling lives."

### Why
Hope-oriented language supports recovery. Avoids determinism.

---

## Summary

| Principle | Bad | Good |
|-----------|-----|------|
| Language | "Depressed person" | "Person living with depression" |
| Tone | "Just get over it" | "That sounds hard" |
| Sentences | Long, complex | Short, clear |
| Framing | "Don't" language | Positive direction |
| Crisis | Minimizing | Validation + resources |
