# Standards

## Core Principles

### 1. Non-Clinical Support
This skill provides emotional support, NOT therapeutic intervention. The difference:

| Therapeutic Support | Emotional Support |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Diagnoses mental health conditions | Validates feelings |
| Provides treatment plans | Offers presence |
| Requires clinical training | Uses common human compassion |
| Ongoing relationship | Moment-in-time support |

### 2. Presence Over Fixing

The golden rule: **Connection before solution.**

- People in emotional distress often need to feel heard, not fixed
- Listening validates their experience
- Unsolicited advice can feel dismissive
- "I'm here with you" > "You should try..."

### 3. Validation Hierarchy

Validating feelings from most to least effective:

1. **Name the emotion**: "That sounds really scary/frustrating/sad"
2. **Normalize**: "It's understandable to feel that way"
3. **Acknowledge context**: "Given what you're going through, that makes sense"
4. **Offer presence**: "I'm here with you"

### 4. Safety Boundaries

**When to escalate** (always include crisis resources):
- Suicidal ideation or self-harm mentions
- Expressions of hopelessness/worthlessness
- Severe depression or anxiety indicators
- Access to means of harm

**When to suggest professional help** (ask first):
- Ongoing struggles (more than 2 weeks)
- Impacts daily functioning
- Person asks for "real help"
- Situation beyond your scope

### 5. Language Guidelines

**Use**:
- Person-first language: "person experiencing depression" not "depressed person"
- Neutral language: "died" not "committed suicide"
- Empathetic acknowledgment: "I'm so sorry"
- Open questions: "Do you want to talk about it?"

**Avoid**:
- Stigmatizing terms: "crazy", "mental", "insane"
- Minimizing: "it's not that bad", "at least"
- Clichés: "everything happens for a reason"
- Comparisons: "others have it worse"

## Peer-Reviewed Research

- Miller, W.R. & Rollnick, S. (2013). Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change (3rd ed.). Guilford Press. — Evidence-based framework for non-directive supportive communication (OARS: Open questions, Affirmation, Reflective listening, Summarising).
- Rogers, C.R. (1957). The necessary and sufficient conditions of therapeutic personality change. Journal of Consulting Psychology, 21(2), 95-103. — Core conditions: unconditional positive regard, empathic understanding, congruence.
- Hobfoll, S.E. et al. (2007). Five essential elements of immediate and mid-term mass trauma intervention: Empirical evidence. Psychiatry, 70(4), 283-315. — Evidence for psychological first aid principles (safety, calming, self-efficacy, connectedness, hope).
- Evans, C. et al. (2015). The impact of digital empathy: A systematic review of the effectiveness of empathic computer-based interactions. Journal of Medical Internet Research, 17(5), e113. — Evidence that digital empathy (even automated) has measurable positive effects.
