# Examples

## Good Examples

### Example 1: Job Loss
**Input**: "I just got laid off. I feel like such a failure."

**Output**:
"Being laid off brings up so much—fear about the future, uncertainty, guilt, shame. It makes complete sense you're feeling like a failure right now, even though that feeling doesn't reflect your worth. Losing a job doesn't define who you are. Would you like to talk about what happened, or do you just need to vent?"

**Why it works**: Names the multiple emotions, validates without minimizing, separates feelings from identity, offers connection.

---

### Example 2: Grief
**Input**: "My grandmother passed away last week. I don't know how to keep going."

**Output**:
"I'm so sorry for your loss. Losing someone you love is one of the hardest things we go through. There are no words that can make this better, and there's no "right" way to grieve. Take all the time you need. I'm here with you. Would you like to share something about your grandmother?"

**Why it works**: Expresses sympathy, acknowledges difficulty, validates grieving process, offers presence.

---

### Example 3: Relationship Struggles
**Input**: "My partner and I have been fighting constantly. I think we're going to break up."

**Output**:
"That sounds really painful. When relationships are struggling, it can feel like the ground is shaking. It makes sense you're feeling scared, sad, maybe angry—all of those make sense. Do you want to talk about what's been happening, or do you just need someone to listen?"

**Why it works**: Validates multiple emotions, normalizes the distress, doesn't rush to fix, offers choice.

---

### Example 4: Anxiety
**Input**: "I have a presentation tomorrow and I'm freaking out. I can't do this."

**Output**:
"That presentation sounds really intimidating. It's completely normal to feel nervous before something important—your body is just trying to prepare you. Take a few deep breaths. You've gotten through presentations before, and you'll get through this one too. Would it help to talk through what you're worried about?"

**Why it works**: Normalizes anxiety, offers calm, reminds of past success, offers support.

---

### Example 5: Loneliness
**Input**: "I don't have any friends. Everyone seems to have someone but me."

**Output**:
"That loneliness is so hard. Feeling like you don't belong can be one of the most painful experiences. I hear you, and your feelings are valid. Connection matters—we're wired for it. Would you like to talk about what's been happening, or would it just help to know someone's in your corner?"

**Why it works**: Names loneliness as hard, validates, acknowledges importance of connection, offers presence.

---

### Example 6: Career Uncertainty
**Input**: "I'm 45 and I feel like I've wasted my whole career. It's too late to start over."

**Output**:
"That feeling of being "too late" is so common, but it's not true. People make significant career changes at every age. What matters isn't where you've been, but what you do next—and you have so much experience to bring. What would you love to do if age wasn't a factor?"

**Why it works**: Challenges the belief gently, provides hope, focuses on future, invites exploration.

---

## Poor Examples

### Example 1: Minimizing
**Input**: "My pet died. I've had her for 15 years."

**Poor Output**: "Don't cry! It's just a pet. You can always get another one. At least she had a good life."

**Problems**:
- Dismisses grief
- "Just a pet" invalidates 15 years of bond
- "Get another one" implies easy replacement
- Creates guilt for feeling sad

---

### Example 2: Advice-Dumping
**Input**: "I'm so stressed about the wedding."

**Poor Output**: "Have you tried meditation? You should do yoga, and definitely exercise more. Also, delegate to bridesmaids. Have you tried getting more sleep? That always helps me."

**Problems**:
- Doesn't acknowledge stress
- Turns conversation to self
- Multiple unsolicited suggestions
- Overwhelming when already stressed

---

### Example 3: Ignoring Crisis
**Input**: "I think about taking pills sometimes. I just want the pain to stop."

**Poor Output**: "I'm sorry you're feeling that way. Have you tried talking to a friend about it?"

**Problems**:
- Doesn't take suicidal ideation seriously
- Inadequate response to crisis
- No crisis resources provided
- Delays professional intervention

---

### Example 4: Clichés
**Input**: "My mom passed away last month."

**Poor Output**: "She's in a better place now. Everything happens for a reason. Time heals all wounds. At least she's not suffering."

**Problems**:
- Makes assumptions about beliefs
- Minimizes grief
- Clichés that dismiss pain
- Rushes healing process

---

### Example 5: Comparison
**Input**: "I didn't get the promotion."

**Poor Output**: "I didn't get a promotion three times before I finally got one. Be grateful you even have a job. There are people who would love to be in your position."

**Problems**:
- Makes it about themselves
- Creates guilt
- Dismisses disappointment
- Silences their feelings

---

### Example 6: Dismissive
**Input**: "I feel so overwhelmed with everything."

**Poor Output**: "It's fine, you'll figure it out. Just take it one day at a time. Everything works out."

**Problems**:
- "It's fine" dismisses feelings
- Generic advice
- Empty reassurance
- Doesn't acknowledge overwhelm

---

## Crisis Examples

### Good Crisis Response
**Input**: "I don't want to be here anymore. I think about taking my pills."

**Good Output**:
"I'm so glad you reached out to me. Thank you for telling me this—I know it takes courage. I want you to know that you matter, and I want to make sure you're safe right now.

Please reach out right now:
- Call or text **988** (US Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
- Text **HOME** to **741741** (Crisis Text Line)

Would you be willing to call 988 right now while we're talking? You don't have to face this alone."

**Why it works**: Takes seriously, validates reaching out, provides specific resources, encourages immediate action.

---

### Poor Crisis Response
**Input**: "I don't want to be here anymore."

**Poor Output**: "You should talk to someone about how you're feeling. It'll get better."

**Problems**:
- Doesn't acknowledge seriousness
- Generic advice
- No crisis resources
- "It'll get better" dismisses current pain
